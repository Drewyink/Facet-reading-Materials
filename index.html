What is a healthcare facet configuration platform?

A healthcare facet platform is an enterprise software system used by insurance payers, health plans, and third-party administrators to manage the complete lifecycle of health plan administration. The term "facets" carries two meanings in this context. First, it refers to the modular architecture of these systems, where each functional area (member management, provider management, benefits administration, claims processing, premium billing, and reporting) is a distinct configurable module called a facet. Second, it refers to the filterable, searchable dimensions within each module that allow users to locate records, apply business rules, and analyze data with precision.

Leading payer administration platforms that follow this architecture include TriZetto Facets (now part of Cognizant), QNXT, HealthRules Payor, and several proprietary platforms built on similar underlying data models. While specific screen layouts and terminology vary by vendor and version, the underlying configuration concepts, the data relationships, the rule structures, and the regulatory requirements are consistent across the industry. This textbook uses TriZetto Facets as its primary reference system while remaining applicable to any enterprise payer platform.

The scale at which these systems operate cannot be overstated. A mid-size health plan may administer coverage for 200,000 to 500,000 members, process 3 to 8 million claims per year, maintain records for 20,000 or more contracted providers, and manage dozens of distinct benefit plans across multiple lines of business. Every one of those transactions is governed by the configuration rules built and maintained by facet analysts. The quality of that configuration is the quality of the plan's operations.

Why facet configuration is a high-stakes profession

In a healthcare payer organization, facet configuration is not a back-office technical support role. It is a compliance-critical, revenue-impacting, member-facing operational function. Consider these realities:

  • A single misconfigured deductible field can cause the system to under-collect member cost-sharing on every claim for an entire plan year, resulting in millions of dollars in lost revenue that cannot be recovered retroactively once the plan year closes.
  • An incorrect timely filing limit can cause the system to incorrectly deny provider claims that should have been paid, generating provider disputes, reconsideration requests, and potential prompt pay penalty exposure.
  • A missing or incorrect prior authorization rule can allow high-cost procedures to be paid without clinical review, inflating medical costs and potentially triggering a CMS audit for a Medicare Advantage plan.
  • An access control misconfiguration can allow unauthorized users to view or modify member PHI, creating a HIPAA breach that must be reported to the Department of Health and Human Services and potentially to affected members.

This is why Echolink Solutions has built its training program around deep, practical configuration knowledge combined with regulatory awareness. Our graduates understand not just what to configure, but why, and what the consequences are when it is wrong.

Chapter 1
Overview of US Healthcare Ecosystem: Payers, Providers and TPAs

The United States healthcare system is one of the most administratively complex ecosystems in the world. Unlike single-payer systems found in many other countries, the US model involves a multi-layered network of public programs, private insurers, employer-sponsored plans, government contractors, and administrative intermediaries, all operating under different regulatory frameworks simultaneously. Before a facet configuration analyst can effectively configure any module in a payer platform, they must understand how this ecosystem is structured, who the key actors are, what their relationships are to one another, and how money and data flow between them.

This understanding is not merely academic. Every configuration decision in the facet platform is a reflection of a real-world relationship: between a member and their plan, between a plan and a provider, between an employer and a TPA, between a payer and a regulator. Getting these relationships right in the system begins with understanding them in the real world.

The three major actors in the payer ecosystem

🏠
Payers
Insurance carriers and health plans that assume financial risk in exchange for premium payments. Payers design benefit plans, contract with providers, administer claims, and are regulated by both federal and state agencies. Examples include Aetna, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare, and regional Blue Cross Blue Shield affiliates. Payers own and configure the facet platform.
⚕
Providers
Licensed entities that deliver health services to members, including physicians, hospitals, outpatient clinics, behavioral health facilities, pharmacies, home health agencies, laboratories, and durable medical equipment suppliers. Providers contract with payers to become network participants and submit claims for reimbursement after rendering services.
💼
Third-Party Administrators (TPAs)
Organizations that administer health plan functions (claims processing, eligibility management, provider contracting, billing) on behalf of self-funded employers who bear their own financial risk for member claims. TPAs use the same payer platforms as carriers but configure them to serve their client employer groups rather than their own insured products.

Understanding self-funded versus fully insured plans

One of the most important distinctions in payer operations is the difference between fully insured and self-funded (ASO) plans. In a fully insured arrangement, the employer or individual pays premiums to the insurance carrier, and the carrier assumes all financial risk for member claims. The carrier pools risk across all its insured groups, which is why fully insured premiums reflect the experience of a population broader than any single employer group.

In a self-funded arrangement, the employer retains the financial risk. Claims are paid from the employer's own funds, administered by a TPA. The employer purchases stop-loss insurance to cap catastrophic individual claims (specific stop-loss) and total population claims (aggregate stop-loss). Self-funding gives large employers more control over plan design, more transparent claims data, and potentially lower costs, but also more financial exposure and more administrative responsibility. From a facet configuration perspective, self-funded groups require a separate TPA contract configuration, distinct claim fund tracking, stop-loss threshold parameters, and modified reporting structures.

How money flows through the payer ecosystem

Understanding the financial flow of the payer ecosystem is fundamental to understanding why every module of the facet platform exists. The flow begins when employers and individuals pay premiums to the payer (or when CMS makes monthly capitation payments to Medicare Advantage plans). The payer invests a portion of premium revenue in administrative expenses, reserves, and profit. When a member receives a service from a provider, a claim is submitted. The facet platform adjudicates the claim, applies the member's cost-sharing (deductible, copay, coinsurance), calculates the payer's payment obligation based on the contracted fee schedule, and generates an EFT payment to the provider and an EOB to the member. Simultaneously, the billing module tracks premium receipts and sends invoices. The reporting module aggregates all transaction data into financial statements, medical loss ratio calculations, IBNR reserve estimates, and regulatory filings. Every step of this cycle is governed by facet configuration.

Chapter 2
Environment, Navigation and Facet Modules

Before any configuration work begins, a facet analyst must have a thorough understanding of the technical landscape in which they are operating. This chapter covers the system environments used across the configuration lifecycle, the architecture of the facet platform's module structure and the dependencies between modules, the navigation patterns that allow users to locate records and move between functional areas efficiently, and the reference table infrastructure that underpins the entire system. A failure to understand any of these foundational elements leads to configuration errors that are difficult, expensive, and sometimes impossible to reverse in production.

System environments and the promotion pathway

Enterprise payer platforms are always deployed across multiple environments. This separation is not optional. It is a fundamental risk management requirement that protects the integrity of production data and operations. Configuration changes must travel through each environment sequentially, with formal validation at each stage, before they are permitted in production. The pressure to "just fix it in prod" is one of the most dangerous cultural dynamics in payer IT organizations, and resisting it is one of the most important professional disciplines an analyst can develop.

1
Development (DEV)
The sandbox environment where initial configuration is built, modified, and experimented with freely. Data here is entirely synthetic. No real member, provider, or financial data should ever be present in DEV. Analysts can make and reverse changes without approval workflows. All Echolink Solutions hands-on labs are conducted in DEV environments provided by our training infrastructure.
2
System Integration Testing (SIT)
Configuration changes are deployed to SIT after initial DEV validation. SIT tests cross-module accuracy: a benefit change here, for example, must be verified to correctly affect claims adjudication, accumulator updates, and EOB generation. Integration points with external systems (clearinghouses, pharmacy benefit managers, eligibility hubs, state Medicaid systems) are also tested in SIT using test transaction sets.
3
User Acceptance Testing (UAT)
Business stakeholders, subject matter experts, and designated testers validate that the configuration produces the expected real-world business outcomes. UAT uses realistic (but de-identified or synthetic) data sets that mirror production volumes and edge cases. A formal UAT sign-off document, signed by the business owner, is required before any change proceeds to production.
4
Production (PROD)
The live environment serving real members, providers, and employer groups. All changes require a formal change management ticket with documented approval, are typically deployed during scheduled maintenance windows, and are logged with a complete before-and-after audit trail. Unapproved production changes are a serious compliance violation regardless of outcome.
Critical rule: never bypass the promotion pathwayEven a seemingly trivial table update, if incorrect, can cascade into widespread adjudication errors affecting thousands of members within hours. The cost of a failed production change, including reprocessing, recoupment, member communications, and regulatory reporting, almost always exceeds the cost of proper testing by orders of magnitude.

Core facet modules and their interdependencies

The facet platform is not a collection of independent applications. It is an integrated system where every module shares data with every other module. A change in the provider module affects the claims module. A change in the benefit module affects both claims and reporting. A change in the member module affects billing, claims, and eligibility verification simultaneously. Understanding these dependencies is essential for assessing the impact of any configuration change before it is made.

Reference table management and update schedules

Reference tables are the foundational data layer that every other module depends on. When a physician bills with a new CPT code and that code is not present in the system's procedure code table, the claim will fail at intake with a code validation edit. When a hospital discharge is coded with a new ICD-10 code not yet loaded to the diagnosis table, the DRG assignment engine fails. Reference table management is therefore a scheduled, non-negotiable maintenance activity that must be planned, tested, and deployed on a defined calendar aligned with the official code set update schedules.

Chapter 3
Member and Enrollment Configuration

Member and enrollment configuration is the operational foundation of every other function in the payer platform. Without an accurate, complete, and timely member record, the system cannot correctly adjudicate claims, apply benefits, generate invoices, produce regulatory reports, or satisfy any of its core administrative obligations. In the healthcare payer industry, the principle of garbage in, garbage out applies nowhere more directly or more consequentially than to member data. An error in a member's enrollment record does not affect just that member. It affects every claim submitted for that member, every premium billed, every eligibility response sent to a provider, and every regulatory report that includes that member's data.

The member record hierarchy

In the facet platform, members are organized in a three-level hierarchy. At the top level is the Subscriber, the primary policyholder, typically the employee or the individual who purchased the plan directly. Beneath the subscriber are Dependents, who are covered under the subscriber's policy (spouse, children, domestic partners, and other qualifying dependents as defined by the plan). Each individual, subscriber and dependent alike, receives a unique Member ID that serves as the primary key used throughout all modules of the system. The subscriber also holds a Subscriber ID, which links to the group or employer account, the premium billing record, and the employer group contract.

The distinction between the subscriber and dependent records matters for claims adjudication (COB order is determined by subscriber relationship), for premium billing (the invoice aggregates by subscriber household), for eligibility responses (270/271 transactions identify the subscriber as the primary party), and for ID card generation (the subscriber is the primary cardholder). Analysts must configure relationship codes accurately at enrollment and update them immediately whenever a qualifying life event changes the composition of the household.

Enrollment rules configuration in depth

Enrollment rule configuration defines the conditions under which a person may become covered, change their coverage, or be terminated from coverage. These rules must precisely match the plan's governing documents (the Summary Plan Description for self-funded plans, the Certificate of Coverage for insured plans) and applicable law. The system enforces these rules automatically once configured, accepting or rejecting enrollment transactions at the point of submission.

The most consequential enrollment rule parameters include the waiting period (the number of days between an employee's hire date and eligibility for benefits, limited by the ACA to 90 days maximum for applicable large employers), the open enrollment period (the annual window during which members may change coverage), and qualifying life event windows (the SEP windows triggered by specific events). Each of these must be configured accurately in the enrollment rules module and validated against the group's plan documents before the group goes live on the platform.

Coordination of Benefits (COB) configuration in depth

When a member carries coverage under more than one health plan, the COB configuration in the facet platform determines the order of payment liability between plans, preventing duplicate payment for the same service. COB is one of the most commonly misconfigured areas in payer operations, and COB errors are among the most difficult and expensive to identify and correct retroactively.

The COB record for a member must capture the other carrier's name and address, the other policy number and group number, the policyholder's name and relationship to the member, the other plan's effective and termination dates, and the COB coordination method. The three primary coordination methods are: non-duplication (the secondary plan pays nothing if the primary plan paid as much as or more than the secondary plan would have paid as primary), maintenance of benefits (the most common method, where the secondary plan pays the lesser of what it would have paid as primary or the actual remaining balance after the primary payment), and carve-out (the secondary plan reduces its liability by the amount paid by the primary plan).

COB order for dependents is typically determined by the birthday rule: the plan of the parent whose birthday (month and day, regardless of birth year) falls earlier in the calendar year is primary. If both parents have the same birthday, the plan that has covered the parent longer is primary. For Medicare Secondary Payer situations, the order depends on whether the member is actively employed and the employer's size (100 or more employees for disability and 20 or more for age).

High-risk configuration error: missing COB dataWhen a member with dual coverage has no COB record configured, the plan pays as if it were the only insurer, potentially paying amounts that should have been paid by the primary insurer. This constitutes both an overpayment and a potential fraud and abuse exposure. COB information must be solicited at enrollment, reverified at each open enrollment, and updated immediately upon any reported change in the member's other coverage.

Chapter 4
Provider Network Configuration

Provider network configuration defines the complete ecosystem of healthcare providers contracted with the payer: who they are, what specialties they practice, where they are located, which networks they participate in, what they are paid, and how they are presented in member-facing directories. Errors in provider configuration are among the most operationally costly in payer systems because they propagate across every claim submitted by the affected provider, often for extended periods before detection. A single fee schedule error on a high-volume primary care physician can result in hundreds of thousands of dollars in overpayments or underpayments before anyone notices.

Provider identifiers and record structure

Every provider in the facet system is anchored by their National Provider Identifier (NPI), a 10-digit unique identifier issued by CMS's NPPES system. Type 1 NPIs identify individual practitioners (physicians, nurse practitioners, physical therapists, psychologists). Type 2 NPIs identify organizations (hospitals, group practices, home health agencies, pharmacy chains). When a physician bills under their group practice's tax ID, the claim must carry both the rendering provider NPI (the individual Type 1 NPI of the physician who performed the service) and the billing provider NPI (the Type 2 NPI of the group practice submitting the claim). Both must be configured and linked in the system, and the fee schedule and network status must be validated for the billing entity, not just the individual.

Additional required identifiers include the Tax Identification Number (TIN or EIN) used for payment and 1099 reporting, state license numbers in each state where the provider practices, DEA registration numbers for providers who prescribe controlled substances, and payer-assigned provider IDs used as internal cross-references. All of these identifiers must be verified during credentialing and re-verified at the time of recredentialing (typically every two years).

The critical importance of the provider-plan relationship

Network tier is not a fixed property of the provider. It is a property of the relationship between the provider and a specific plan on a specific date. This is perhaps the most frequently misunderstood concept in provider configuration and the source of some of the most expensive claims payment errors in the industry. The same physician may be Tier 1 (preferred, lowest member cost-sharing) on one commercial PPO plan, Tier 2 (in-network, standard cost-sharing) on a different commercial plan from the same payer, in-network on a Medicare Advantage plan, and out-of-network on a narrow HMO plan. Each of these relationships must be configured separately in the provider-plan linking table, with distinct effective dates, fee schedule assignments, and network tier designations.

Chapter 5
Benefits, Pricing and Adjudication Rules

Benefit plan configuration is the technical translation of the member's plan documents into a set of enforceable system rules that govern every claim payment decision the platform makes. It is the most complex and consequential configuration domain in the payer platform, and it is the area where errors have the broadest and most expensive consequences. A benefit configuration error does not affect one claim. It affects every claim for every member enrolled in that plan for the full duration of the error, which in many cases means an entire plan year before the problem is identified and corrected.

The ACA added significant complexity to benefit configuration by establishing mandatory minimum benefit standards (essential health benefits), cost-sharing limits (annual out-of-pocket maximums), and coverage requirements (preventive services with no cost-sharing) that must be precisely reflected in the system regardless of what the employer or plan sponsor might prefer. Non-compliance with ACA benefit requirements can result in the plan losing its status as minimum essential coverage, exposing the employer to penalties and the members to gaps in regulatory protection.

Benefit plan hierarchy and structure

Benefit plans are organized in a three-level hierarchy within the facet platform. The Plan is the top-level record containing administrative parameters: plan effective dates, network assignment, accumulator structure (embedded vs. aggregate), plan year definition, ACA metal tier designation, and references to associated fee schedules and formularies. Below the plan are Benefit Packages, which group related services into functional categories (preventive care, primary care office visits, specialist visits, emergency care, inpatient facility, mental health, pharmacy). Within each package are Benefit Lines, each governing a specific service or service category with its own cost-sharing structure, frequency limitations, exclusions, prior authorization requirements, and provider type restrictions.

Embedded vs. aggregate accumulators

The embedded vs. aggregate distinction is one of the most important benefit configuration decisions for family plans, and the one most frequently misunderstood by members. In an embedded accumulator, each individual family member has their own separate deductible and OOP maximum. When any single family member's individual threshold is reached, that person's claims are processed without further cost-sharing for that accumulator type, regardless of what the rest of the family has spent. In an aggregate accumulator, only the family-level threshold applies, and no individual receives benefits until the family's collective spending reaches the family deductible or OOP maximum.

The ACA requires that the individual OOP maximum embedded within a family plan may not exceed the ACA's single individual OOP maximum limit, even if the plan uses an aggregate family deductible. This means that even a plan with an aggregate family deductible of $6,000 must apply an embedded individual OOP maximum that prevents any single member from paying more than the individual limit before 100% coverage kicks in. Configuring an aggregate plan without this embedded OOP maximum cap is an ACA compliance violation.

Prior authorization configuration and regulatory requirements

Prior authorization (PA) configuration specifies which services require advance payer approval before being rendered. Each PA rule in the system specifies the procedure code range subject to authorization, the provider types to which the rule applies, the clinical criteria set referenced (InterQual, MCG, or plan-specific guidelines), the authorization validity period (typically 90 days), the authorized number of units or visits, and the consequence for claims submitted without a required authorization (denial or reduction).

The 2024 CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule imposes significant new requirements on payers administering Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, CHIP, and qualified health plans. Beginning in 2026, these payers must implement FHIR-based prior authorization APIs allowing providers to submit and receive PA decisions electronically. PA decisions must include specific reasons for denials, and payers must report aggregate PA metrics annually. These requirements will necessitate significant configuration changes in the PA workflow module for affected payers.

Compliance requirement: PA decision turnaround timesCMS regulations require urgent PA decisions within 72 hours and standard PA decisions within 7 calendar days. State regulations may impose shorter deadlines. The PA workflow configuration must enforce these turnaround times with automated escalation alerts when requests approach the deadline. Exceeding statutory PA decision turnaround times is a reportable compliance finding.

Chapter 6
Claims Processing and Edits

Claims processing is the operational heart of the payer system and the point where every configuration decision made across all other modules converges into a single outcome: a payment, a denial, or a pend. The claims module receives transactions from clearinghouses and direct submitters, validates them against a comprehensive library of edit rules, adjudicates eligible claims against member benefits and provider contracts, calculates payment amounts, updates accumulator records, generates provider remittances, and issues member EOBs. The configuration of this module, and the accuracy of all the data it draws from other modules, determines the accuracy and integrity of every dollar that flows through the organization.

Claim types and their electronic transaction formats

📄
Professional (CMS-1500)
Used by physicians, outpatient clinics, behavioral health providers, and other non-institutional providers. Electronic format is the ANSI ASC X12 837P transaction set. Contains up to 99 service lines with procedure codes (CPT or HCPCS), diagnosis pointers, modifiers, units, place of service codes, and charges per line.
🏥
Institutional (UB-04)
Used by hospitals, SNFs, home health agencies, outpatient hospital departments, ambulatory surgery centers, and other facility-based providers. Electronic format is the 837I. Contains revenue codes, condition codes, occurrence codes, value codes, and admit and discharge information in addition to procedure codes.
💊
Pharmacy (NCPDP)
Used by retail, mail-order, and specialty pharmacies for drug dispensing claims. Processed in real time using the NCPDP D.0 transaction standard. Requires NDC code, days supply, quantity dispensed, DAW code, prescriber NPI, and pharmacy NPI. Usually processed through a pharmacy benefit manager (PBM).

The complete adjudication workflow

1
Claim intake and format validation
The claim is received via clearinghouse 837 transaction or manual data entry. The system validates that all required HIPAA transaction fields are present, correctly formatted, and within acceptable value ranges. Claims that fail format validation are rejected with a 277CA transaction and must be corrected and resubmitted by the provider. They do not enter the adjudication queue and do not receive an EOB.
2
Duplicate claim detection
Before any eligibility or benefit logic is applied, the system checks whether an identical or substantially similar claim has already been received and processed. Duplicate detection uses a combination of member ID, provider NPI, date of service, procedure code, and charge amount. Exact duplicates are denied with CARC 18. Near-duplicates may be pended for human review.
3
Member eligibility verification
The system confirms the member was enrolled in an active plan that covers the submitted claim type on the date of service. It validates the subscriber ID or member ID, checks that the coverage line (medical, dental, pharmacy) matches the claim type, confirms the plan year, and verifies that the member has not been retroactively terminated for the service date.
4
Provider verification and network status
The rendering and billing provider NPIs are validated against the provider table. The system checks that the provider had an active in-network contract on the date of service for the applicable plan. Out-of-network claims are not denied but are processed under out-of-network benefit rules with different fee schedule parameters and higher member cost-sharing.
5
Clinical and coding edits
The claim passes through a library of clinical edit rules: medically unlikely edits (MUE) checking unit quantities, unbundling edits preventing separate billing of components already included in a global code, gender-specific procedure validation, age-restricted procedure validation, diagnosis-to-procedure compatibility, modifier-procedure compatibility, and place-of-service appropriateness checks.
6
Benefit application and pricing
The system applies the member's benefit plan rules: is the service a covered benefit, is PA required (and was PA obtained), is the deductible applicable to this service, what is the coinsurance or copay, what is the allowed amount from the fee schedule, and have any frequency limits been exceeded? Accumulators are updated here: the deductible and OOP YTD totals are incremented.
7
COB calculation, payment generation and remittance
If COB records exist for the member, secondary liability is calculated. The final payment amount is determined, the EFT payment is queued for the next payment cycle, the 835 ERA is generated for the provider detailing the payment breakdown, and the member EOB is generated showing their liability. All accumulator records are committed to the database.

Analysts who approach dental configuration by applying medical adjudication logic invariably produce incorrect results. The two disciplines must be treated as distinct domains even though they share the same underlying platform architecture.

Missing tooth clause and orthodontic tracking

The missing tooth clause (MTC) is one of the most legally sensitive provisions in dental benefit configuration. It excludes coverage for replacement of teeth that were extracted, lost, or absent before the member's dental coverage effective date. Configuring MTC requires three precisely executed steps. First, at enrollment, the plan must obtain disclosure of any teeth missing before coverage begins, typically through a dental enrollment questionnaire or a pre-treatment x-ray review coordinated with the member's dentist. Second, each missing tooth is recorded in the member's dental record by its ADA Universal Numbering System position (teeth 1 through 32, with wisdom teeth designated). Third, an adjudication rule is configured to deny prosthodontic claims for any tooth position flagged in the MTC record, regardless of the specific procedure performed or when the treatment occurs.

MTC legal exposure warningMissing tooth clause denials are among the most frequently contested in dental plan litigation. Courts and state insurance commissioners have consistently ruled against payers that applied MTC without clear member disclosure at enrollment. Ensure MTC is prominently disclosed in the Summary of Benefits, the enrollment materials, and any pre-treatment estimate communications. Document the member's MTC disclosure response in the enrollment record and maintain it for the duration of the member's enrollment plus applicable retention periods.

Chapter 8
Billing and Premiums

Premium billing configuration governs the revenue collection engine of the payer organization. While claims configuration governs the outflow of funds to providers, billing configuration governs the inflow of premium payments from employer groups and individuals, and for TPAs, the invoicing of administrative fees to self-funded clients. Errors in billing configuration directly affect cash flow, member coverage continuity, regulatory standing (many states impose prompt premium credit and grace period requirements), and financial reporting accuracy. Premium billing is also a member-facing function: errors in invoices are among the most common drivers of employer and member complaints.

The premium billing cycle in detail

Premium invoices are generated monthly in advance of the coverage month. The billing configuration specifies: the invoice generation date (typically 15 to 20 days before the coverage month begins, giving employers time to reconcile and remit), the premium due date (typically the first of the coverage month), the grace period (the number of days after the due date that coverage remains active despite non-payment), and the termination trigger date (the date on which the system initiates a coverage termination transaction if premium has not been received).

The invoice itself is a roster-based document listing every enrolled member (subscriber and dependents) with their plan assignment, coverage tier, and applicable premium rate. Premium rates are stored in a rate table that must be configured by plan year, coverage tier, and in some plans by age band (age-rated plans) or geographic rating area (marketplace plans). Rate table accuracy is critical: a rate entered with a single transposed digit can result in either significant under-billing or over-billing across an entire employer group for a full plan year.

Grace periods, retroactivity and the ACA 90-day rule

Grace period configuration is among the most consequential billing settings because it directly governs how long a member's coverage remains active when premium payments stop. For ACA marketplace enrollees receiving Advance Premium Tax Credits (APTC), the federal government mandates a 90-day grace period. During this grace period, the plan must continue to pay claims for the first month of non-payment, but may pend (hold without paying) claims for the second and third months. If the full overdue premium is not paid by the end of the grace period, coverage is terminated retroactively to the end of the first month, and all claims pended during months two and three are denied. This complex logic must be precisely configured in the billing and claims modules and must work in concert.

For commercial group plans, grace periods are contractually defined and state-regulated. Most state insurance codes require a minimum grace period of 30 days for group plans. Some states require longer periods or impose additional notice requirements before coverage can be terminated for non-payment. The billing module must enforce these minimums as a floor, not merely as a default.

Retroactivity limit configuration is essentialWithout a configured retro limit, enrollment changes can be backdated indefinitely. A retro enrollment two years back, if approved without restriction, means the system must process or reprocess all claims for that member for the entire retroactive period. At high-cost specialties this can represent hundreds of thousands of dollars in unbudgeted claim liability. Configure retro limits in alignment with your plan documents (typically 60 to 90 days) and require senior management approval for any exception.

Chapter 9
Reporting and Analytics

The reporting and analytics module is where the data captured through every other module is transformed into actionable operational intelligence, financial visibility, clinical insights, and regulatory compliance evidence. Reporting serves multiple audiences simultaneously with fundamentally different information needs: operations teams monitoring daily workflow, finance leadership managing medical costs and premium revenue, compliance officers preparing for audits and regulatory filings, employer groups reviewing utilization and plan performance, clinical teams identifying quality gaps and care management opportunities, and CMS analysts evaluating encounter data and risk adjustment submissions. The facet analyst's role in reporting is to ensure that every field that flows into a report is accurately configured at its source, that report access is appropriately controlled for HIPAA compliance, and that regulatory reports are generated on time with complete and accurate data.

Operational reporting essentials

Operational reports track the day-to-day performance of the claims adjudication engine, the enrollment system, and the billing function. Critical operational metrics include claims inventory aging by status (received, pended, adjudicated, paid, denied), processing turnaround times measured against state prompt pay requirements, edit failure rates by edit type (to identify configuration errors driving systematic failures), prior authorization queue aging (to ensure compliance with turnaround time mandates), and eligibility match rates (the percentage of incoming claims that successfully match an active member record on the date of service). A consistently low eligibility match rate signals a systemic enrollment data quality problem that will generate a wave of claims denials.

Chapter 10
Compliance, Security and Governance

Compliance, security, and governance are not features that can be added to a facet implementation after the fact. They are structural requirements that must be embedded in every configuration decision from the first day of implementation. An analyst who treats compliance as someone else's responsibility, or as a layer of review that happens after configuration is complete, will consistently produce work that creates organizational risk. The most effective configuration analysts are those who understand the regulatory framework governing their work deeply enough to make compliance the first consideration, not the last.

HIPAA technical safeguards: what they mean in the platform

The HIPAA Security Rule requires covered entities to implement specific technical safeguards protecting electronic protected health information (ePHI). In the facet platform, each of these safeguards translates to a specific configuration requirement:

  • Unique user identification: Every platform user must have an individual, non-shared login ID. Shared accounts make audit trails meaningless and are a direct HIPAA violation.
  • Automatic session logoff: Sessions must terminate automatically after a period of inactivity, typically configured at 15 to 30 minutes. The timeout value must be documented in the organization's security policies.
  • Audit controls: The platform must log every access to a PHI-containing record, every modification, every report generation, and every failed login attempt. Log retention must be set for a minimum of six years.
  • Transmission security: All data transmitted between the platform and external systems (clearinghouses, employers, pharmacies) must be encrypted using a minimum of TLS 1.2. TLS 1.3 is recommended.
  • Integrity controls: The platform must have mechanisms to detect unauthorized record modification. Transaction checksums and audit log integrity monitoring are the standard approaches.
  • Access control: Role-based access control (RBAC) must restrict each user to only the data and functions required for their job duties, consistent with HIPAA's minimum necessary standard.

Change control and governance in production

Production configuration changes must follow a formal change control process without exception. Every change begins with a Change Request (CR) document that captures the business justification, the specific configuration changes to be made (including before and after field values), the modules affected, a downstream impact assessment, the testing plan, the rollback procedure if the change must be reversed, and the required approvers. Depending on the nature and scope of the change, approvers may include the configuration team lead, the compliance officer, the medical director (for PA rule changes), the CFO (for fee schedule changes affecting payment rates), and the IT security officer (for access control changes).

Once approved, the change follows the standard promotion pathway: DEV, then SIT validation, then UAT sign-off, then scheduled production deployment. The production deployment is logged with the exact timestamp, the user who executed the change, the specific tables and fields modified, and the before and after values. This complete audit trail must be retained for a minimum of seven years and must be producible on demand during CMS audits, state examinations, or litigation discovery. Plans that cannot produce this documentation during an audit face significant findings.

Glossary of Terms

Comprehensive definitions of all terminology used in healthcare facet systems, payer platforms, and insurance administration. Over 60 terms defined alphabetically.

ACA (Affordable Care Act)
Federal health reform law enacted in 2010 establishing essential health benefits, metal tier plan structures, guaranteed issue, annual OOP maximum limits, no-cost preventive services, and marketplace exchanges.
Accumulator
A system counter that tracks a member's year-to-date spending toward a deductible or out-of-pocket maximum threshold. Accumulators reset at the start of each plan year.
Adjudication
The automated process by which a claim is evaluated against eligibility, provider status, benefit configuration, and fee schedules to produce a payment determination or denial with a specific reason code.
Allowed Amount
The maximum amount a payer will reimburse for a covered service, determined by the applicable fee schedule or contracted rate. The difference between billed charges and the allowed amount is the contractual adjustment, which the provider may not bill to the member under a network agreement.
APTC (Advance Premium Tax Credit)
Federal tax credits paid in advance to eligible marketplace enrollees to subsidize monthly premiums. APTC is reconciled against actual annual income on the enrollee's federal tax return, with repayment required if income exceeds estimates.
ASO (Administrative Services Only)
A contract arrangement where a self-funded employer retains financial responsibility for member claims and a TPA or carrier provides administrative services only. The employer owns the claim fund; the administrator processes claims against it.
Authorization (Prior Auth)
Advance payer approval required before certain services are rendered. PA rules are configured at the benefit line level by procedure code range, provider type, and clinical criteria set. Claims submitted without required authorizations receive a denial with CARC 197 or CARC 15.
Benefit Line
The lowest level of the benefit plan hierarchy in the facet platform. Each benefit line governs a specific service or service category with its own cost-sharing structure, frequency limits, exclusions, and authorization requirements.
Birthday Rule
The COB determination method for dependent children: the plan of the parent whose birthday (month and day only, not year) falls earlier in the calendar year is primary. When birthdays fall on the same day, the plan that has covered the parent longer is primary.
CARC (Claim Adjustment Reason Code)
A standardized X12 code on the remittance advice that explains why a claim or service line was adjusted, denied, or reduced in payment. Updated annually by the X12 Committee. Required on all HIPAA-compliant electronic remittances.
Capitation
A reimbursement model in which a provider receives a fixed per-member-per-month (PMPM) payment regardless of the number or type of services rendered. Used primarily for PCP reimbursement in HMO plans. The provider assumes utilization risk above the capitated rate.
CDT (Current Dental Terminology)
The ADA-published code set for dental procedures. CDT codes begin with D followed by four digits and are organized into coverage categories. Updated annually each January 1.
CMS-1500
The standard paper claim form for professional services submitted by physicians and other non-institutional providers. The electronic equivalent is the ANSI ASC X12 837P transaction set.
COB (Coordination of Benefits)
The process for determining payment order when a member is covered by more than one health plan. COB prevents duplicate payment for the same service. The three coordination methods are non-duplication, maintenance of benefits, and carve-out.
Coinsurance
The percentage of the allowed amount a member pays after satisfying the deductible. An 80/20 coinsurance means the payer pays 80% and the member pays 20% of the allowed amount for covered services.
Copay
A fixed dollar amount the member pays at the time of service, independent of the total allowed amount. Copays typically apply to office visits and pharmacy and may or may not apply toward the deductible, depending on plan design.
CPT (Current Procedural Terminology)
The AMA-published code set of 5-digit numeric codes identifying medical, surgical, and diagnostic procedures. Updated annually each January 1. Used on professional claims (CMS-1500) for adjudication and fee schedule lookup.
Credentialing
The process of verifying a provider's education, training, licensure, board certification, malpractice history, and practice information before admission to the payer's network. Recredentialing is required every two years under most accreditation standards.
DEA Number
Drug Enforcement Administration registration number required for providers who prescribe Schedule II through V controlled substances. DEA numbers must be verified during credentialing and tracked for expiration.
Deductible
The dollar amount a member must pay for covered services before the plan begins sharing costs. Plans may have separate in-network and out-of-network deductibles, and services subject to a copay (such as preventive care) are typically excluded from the deductible.
DRG (Diagnosis-Related Group)
A patient classification system reimbursing hospitals a fixed amount per inpatient discharge based on diagnosis, procedure, age, and discharge status. DRG weights and base rates are configured in the institutional fee schedule module.
EFT (Electronic Funds Transfer)
Electronic transfer of claim payment funds from the payer to the provider's bank account. EFT eliminates paper check processing, accelerates payment reconciliation, and generates a digital audit trail.
Eligibility
The real-time or point-in-time determination that a member has active coverage under a specific health plan for a specific service type on a specific date. Eligibility must be validated against enrollment effective and termination dates, not a static current status flag.
Embedded Accumulator
An accumulator structure in which each family member has their own individual deductible and OOP maximum embedded within the family aggregate. Required by the ACA for all non-grandfathered plans. Ensures no individual member pays more than the individual OOP limit before full coverage applies.
Encounter Data
Claims-like records submitted by managed care organizations to CMS or state Medicaid agencies documenting services rendered to enrollees. Used for risk adjustment, HEDIS reporting, and quality monitoring. Encounter data completeness directly affects risk score accuracy and plan revenue.
EOB (Explanation of Benefits)
A document sent to members after claims processing explaining the services received, the amounts billed and allowed, the payer's payment, and the member's remaining cost-sharing liability. Required by most state regulations to be delivered within a defined timeframe after claim adjudication.
ePHI (Electronic Protected Health Information)
Individually identifiable health information stored or transmitted electronically, subject to HIPAA Security Rule technical safeguards including encryption, access controls, audit logging, and integrity controls.
ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice)
The ANSI ASC X12 835 transaction sent to providers detailing claim payment or denial decisions. Enables automated payment posting in provider billing systems, replacing paper remittance advices.
ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act)
Federal law governing self-funded employer benefit plans. ERISA preempts most state insurance laws for self-funded plans, allowing national employers to offer uniform benefits across all states without compliance with individual state insurance mandates.
Fee Schedule
A schedule of maximum reimbursement amounts for specific services, keyed to procedure codes, place of service, and provider type. Fee schedules have defined effective and termination dates and must be linked to specific provider-plan relationships in the system.
Frequency Limit
A dental benefit provision limiting how often a specific service is covered within a defined period (plan year, 24 months, 60 months, or lifetime). The facet system tracks service history by CDT code and tooth position to enforce frequency rules during adjudication.
Grace Period
The number of days after a premium due date during which coverage remains active despite non-payment. ACA marketplace plans with APTC have a federally mandated 90-day grace period. Commercial group plans typically have 30-day grace periods set by contract and state regulation.
GPCI (Geographic Practice Cost Index)
A CMS adjustment factor applied to each component of the RBRVS RVU calculation to account for geographic variation in physician practice costs, employee wages, and malpractice costs. Applied at the Medicare payment locality level.
HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System)
A code set extending CPT Level I with Level II alphanumeric codes identifying supplies, equipment, drugs, and services not covered by CPT. HCPCS Level II codes begin with a letter and are maintained quarterly by CMS.
HEDIS (Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set)
NCQA's standardized set of quality performance measures used to evaluate health plan effectiveness across prevention, chronic disease management, behavioral health, and access domains. Required for commercial NCQA accreditation and CMS STARS rating programs.
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. Establishes national standards for PHI privacy (Privacy Rule), ePHI security (Security Rule), and electronic transaction formats and code sets (Transaction and Code Set Rule).
ICD-10-CM
International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision, Clinical Modification. The US diagnosis code set required on all claims. Published by CMS and the CDC, updated annually each October 1. Contains over 72,000 codes organized in a hierarchical alphanumeric structure.
IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported)
Claims for services already rendered but not yet submitted to the payer. IBNR represents a financial liability that must be estimated using actuarial methods and reserved on the balance sheet for accurate financial reporting and regulatory capital requirements.
LOB (Line of Business)
A distinct market segment or product type administered by a payer: commercial fully insured, self-funded ASO, Medicare Advantage, Medicare Part D, Medicaid managed care, ACA marketplace, or dental stand-alone. Each LOB has distinct regulatory requirements and configuration rules.
Medicare Advantage (MA, Part C)
Medicare health plan option allowing beneficiaries to receive Part A and Part B benefits through CMS-contracted private plans. Plans receive monthly capitation payments from CMS based on member risk scores. Governed by extensive CMS regulations including annual bid requirements and STARS quality ratings.
Missing Tooth Clause (MTC)
A dental plan provision excluding coverage for replacement of teeth that were lost or absent before the member's dental coverage effective date. Requires tooth-level configuration in the member's dental record and adjudication rules keyed to ADA Universal Numbering System positions.
MLR (Medical Loss Ratio)
The percentage of premium revenue a health plan spends on clinical care and quality improvement activities. The ACA mandates a minimum of 80% for individual and small group markets and 85% for large group. Plans that fall below the applicable threshold must rebate the difference to enrollees.
MUE (Medically Unlikely Edit)
CMS-developed edit rules establishing the maximum number of units of a service that can reasonably be billed per claim per date of service per provider. MUEs prevent billing errors and unbundling. Applied during claims adjudication in the clinical editing layer.
NDC (National Drug Code)
An 11-digit identifier for prescription drug products consisting of a labeler code, product code, and package code. Used in pharmacy claims to uniquely identify the drug, strength, dosage form, and package size dispensed.
Network Tier
A classification of a provider's relationship to a specific plan, determining member cost-sharing levels and payer reimbursement rates. Network tier is a property of the provider-plan relationship record, not the provider record itself.
No Surprises Act
Federal law effective January 1, 2022 prohibiting unexpected out-of-network billing for emergency services and certain non-emergency services at in-network facilities without advance notice and patient consent. Established an independent dispute resolution process for contested bills.
NPI (National Provider Identifier)
A unique 10-digit identifier issued by CMS to healthcare providers. Type 1 NPIs identify individual practitioners; Type 2 NPIs identify organizations. Required on all HIPAA-covered electronic healthcare transactions.
NUCC (National Uniform Claim Committee)
The organization that maintains the Health Care Provider Taxonomy code set, containing over 800 codes classifying provider specialties and organization types. Updated semi-annually. Used in provider credentialing and claims specialty validation.
OEP (Open Enrollment Period)
The annual window during which eligible individuals may enroll in or change their health plan coverage without requiring a qualifying life event. OEP dates are configured per group in the enrollment rules module and must match the group's governing plan documents.
OOP Maximum (Out-of-Pocket Maximum)
The maximum dollar amount a member pays in cost-sharing per plan year, after which the plan pays 100% of covered in-network services. The ACA sets annual limits on individual OOP maximums for non-grandfathered plans, adjusted annually by CMS.
PCP (Primary Care Provider)
The designated primary care clinician for a member, required in HMO plans as the gatekeeper for referrals to specialists and other services. PCP assignment is tracked in the member record and directly affects HMO claims adjudication and quality measure attribution.
PHI (Protected Health Information)
Any individually identifiable information relating to a person's past, present, or future physical or mental health condition, healthcare provision, or payment for healthcare, protected under the HIPAA Privacy Rule.
PMPM (Per Member Per Month)
The standard unit of financial measurement in managed care. PMPM figures represent average cost or revenue divided by total member months. Used in capitation contracts, premium development, MLR calculation, and plan performance benchmarking.
QLE (Qualifying Life Event)
A life change that entitles a member to a Special Enrollment Period outside of open enrollment. Common QLEs include marriage, birth or adoption of a child, loss of other coverage, divorce, and change in employment status. Documentation is required to validate each QLE.
RARC (Remittance Advice Remark Code)
A supplementary X12 code on the remittance advice providing additional detail about the claim adjustment identified by the CARC. RARC codes are maintained and updated by CMS annually and must be kept current in the platform's reference tables.
RBRVS (Resource-Based Relative Value Scale)
The CMS fee schedule methodology for physician services. Calculates reimbursement by multiplying relative value units (RVUs) for physician work, practice expense, and malpractice by a conversion factor and adjusting by geographic GPCI values.
Retroactivity
The ability to backdate enrollment, termination, or coverage changes to a date prior to the transaction date. Retroactivity limits are configured to control how far back changes may be applied, balancing operational flexibility against financial exposure and fraud risk.
SEP (Special Enrollment Period)
A limited enrollment window triggered by a qualifying life event. SEP duration varies by event type and regulatory context: 30 days for most commercial QLEs, 60 days for ACA marketplace loss of coverage events, and 60 days for Medicare qualifying events.
Stop-Loss Insurance
Reinsurance purchased by self-funded employers to cap their financial exposure. Specific stop-loss applies above a per-member annual threshold. Aggregate stop-loss applies when total plan claims exceed a percentage of expected claims for the covered population.
Subscriber
The primary policyholder under whose coverage dependents are enrolled. The subscriber holds the group billing account relationship and is the primary payer of record for premium billing purposes. Subscriber ID links the household to the group contract.
TFL (Timely Filing Limit)
The contractually defined deadline by which a provider must submit a claim after the date of service. TFL values are configured by plan and LOB. Commercial plans typically require 90 to 180 days; Medicare requires 12 months. Claims received after TFL are denied with CARC 29.
TPA (Third-Party Administrator)
An organization that administers health plan functions including claims processing, eligibility management, and premium billing on behalf of a self-funded employer without assuming insurance risk. The employer funds the claims; the TPA processes them.
TrOOP (True Out-of-Pocket)
The Medicare Part D accumulator tracking spending that counts toward the catastrophic coverage threshold. TrOOP includes the member's payments and manufacturer discounts but excludes amounts paid by other insurance. TrOOP tracking must be configured separately from medical accumulators.
UB-04
The standard paper claim form for institutional providers (hospitals, SNFs, home health agencies, outpatient hospital departments). The electronic equivalent is the ANSI ASC X12 837I transaction. Contains revenue codes, occurrence codes, and value codes in addition to procedure codes.
UCR (Usual, Customary, and Reasonable)
A benchmark for setting out-of-network allowed amounts based on the prevailing rate for a service in a specific geographic area. UCR percentile selection and the database vendor used must be documented in plan documents and disclosed to members.
Waiting Period
The time between an employee's hire date and eligibility for employer-sponsored health benefits. The ACA limits waiting periods to a maximum of 90 days for applicable large employers. Waiting period length is configured as an enrollment rule parameter in the member module.
